I built a brain tumor classifier from scratch.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[2/12]

The problem:

Glioma = aggressive malignant tumor → urgent chemo + radiation
Meningioma = typically benign → surgical, watchful waiting

Misclassify one for the other and you don't fail a metric.
You send the wrong patient down the wrong path.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[3/12]

The dataset:

📁 2,800 training MRI scans (1,400 per class)
📁 800 held-out test scans — never touched until final eval
✅ Perfectly balanced
✅ 5-fold stratified cross-validation
✅ Zero data leakage — enforced at the pipeline level

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[4/12]

The architecture — 2 levels:

LEVEL 1: 10 independent CNNs
→ ResNet50V2 × 5 folds
→ DenseNet121 × 5 folds

Each produces P(Glioma), P(Meningioma).
Stack them → shape (N, 20).

LEVEL 2: Logistic Regression meta-learner
→ Trained on out-of-fold predictions

This is stacking. It is more powerful than voting.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[5/12]

Why two different architectures?

ResNet: residual skip connections → global texture
DenseNet: dense feature reuse → fine-grained local features

Networks that FAIL DIFFERENTLY are more valuable than networks that agree.

The meta-learner exploits this. That's the entire point.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[6/12]

MixUp training was non-negotiable.

Instead of training on real images alone, you train on convex combinations:

x̃ = λxᵢ + (1−λ)xⱼ
ỹ = λyᵢ + (1−λ)yⱼ

Forces smoother decision boundaries.
Reduces overconfidence.

Brier score: 0.018. The calibration curve proves it worked.
