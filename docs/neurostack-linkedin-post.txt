I built a brain tumor classifier. It achieved 99.88% accuracy.

Here's what that actually means — and why it matters more than the number.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE PROBLEM

Glioma and Meningioma are two brain tumors with radically different clinical outcomes.

Glioma: aggressive, infiltrative, malignant. Demands immediate chemotherapy.
Meningioma: typically benign, slow-growing. Surgical but manageable.

Confuse one for the other — and you don't fail a benchmark. You send the wrong patient down the wrong treatment path.

Radiologists are brilliant. They're also human. Fatigue, volume pressure, and inter-reader variability are documented problems in neuro-oncology. AI — built rigorously — can be the second opinion that never sleeps.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT I BUILT

NeuroStack: a two-level stacked ensemble.

→ Level 1: 10 CNN base models
   ResNet50V2 × 5 folds + DenseNet121 × 5 folds
   Each trained independently. Each producing its own probability.

→ Level 2: A trained meta-learner
   Logistic Regression on a (N, 20) feature matrix of stacked probabilities.
   It learns how much to trust each model, and when.

This is called stacking. It is fundamentally more powerful than voting.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE RESULTS — on 800 held-out scans the model never saw

✅ Accuracy: 99.88%
✅ Sensitivity: 99.75% — 399 of 400 gliomas caught
✅ Specificity: 100.00% — zero false alarms
✅ Precision: 100.00% — every glioma flag was correct
✅ AUC-ROC: 0.9975
✅ MCC: 0.9975
✅ Cohen's Kappa: 0.9975

The single miss? A glioma case with P(Glioma) ≈ 0.00 — a genuinely borderline scan.

I kept it. Because a model that claims perfection is a model that's lying to you.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE ENGINEERING DECISIONS THAT MADE THE DIFFERENCE

1. Stacking over voting
   Majority voting throws away probability mass. The meta-learner extracts signal from all 20 dimensions.

2. Heterogeneous architectures
   ResNet's residual connections and DenseNet's feature reuse provide complementary inductive biases. Networks that fail differently are more valuable than networks that agree.

3. MixUp training
   Convex combinations of training pairs force smoother decision boundaries. Proven to reduce overconfidence in medical imaging — and it shows in the calibration (Brier: 0.018).

4. Zero data leakage
   Stratified folds, out-of-fold meta-learner training, held-out test set never touched until final evaluation. The results are real.
